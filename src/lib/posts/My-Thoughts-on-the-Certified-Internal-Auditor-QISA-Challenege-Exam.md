## Introduction

About a couple of months ago, I came across the [Certified Internal Auditor (CIA) Challenge Exam for Qualified Information System Auditors (QISA)](https://www.theiia.org/en/promotions/certifications/qisa/qisa-cia-challenge-exam/). Through this route, active CISA holders can obtain their CIA designation after successfully passing a one-part examination focusing on content not covered on your Certified Information Systems Auditor (CISA) exam. This piqued my interest, offering a way to build on my existing understanding of Internal Audit and a strategic move to potentially enhance my professional trajectory.

The QISA Challenge Exam comprises 150 multiple-choice questions covering three core areas of the traditional CIA certification process: Essentials of Internal Auditing, Practice of Internal Auditing, and Business Knowledge for Internal Auditing. With an examination time of up to 180 minutes and a required passing score of 600 (80%) out of 750 points based on a scaled scoring system, it presented a concrete goal to strive towards.

## Exam Preparation

Upon registering for the exam, I was provided with access to the Institute of Internal Auditors (IIA) CIA Learning System which provided reading materials along with quizzes on each section and a practice exam covering all sections. I started studying for the examination two months prior to my testing date, and I believe this was a sufficient amount of time to cover all of the material.

I first started to read through the reading material provided through the learning system. There were three textbooks provided, corresponding to the three sections in the traditional CIA examination process:

- Essentials of Internal Auditing
- Practice of Internal Auditing
- Business Knowledge of Internal Auditing

It took me about a month to get through all of these reading materials. After reading through each textbook, I created an Excel spreadsheet for a more detailed overview of the provided [Syllabus](https://www.theiia.org/globalassets/documents/certifications/challenge-exams/cia-challenge-exam-for-qualified-information-systems-auditors-final.pdf) that was provided, taking care to focus on the sections that required proficiency versus a basic understanding and the expected weighting of each section, like so:

![Certified-Internal-Auditor-Custom-Syllabus](/My-thoughts-on-the-Certified-Internal-Auditor-QISA-Challenge-Exam/CIA-Exam-Custom-Syllabus.png)

This custom study guide allowed me to filter on which areas required a proficient understanding and focus my studying on the most important sections of the exam. I then went through the reading material a second time, but only focusing on the proficient areas and created a custom study guide in Word, noting what seemed to be the key points of each section. This helped, as it took the large amount of text provided within these books, and boiled it down to the key parts which aided my ability to remember and understand.

![Certified-Internal-Auditor-Proficient-Study-Guide](/My-thoughts-on-the-Certified-Internal-Auditor-QISA-Challenge-Exam/CIA-Exam-Proficient-Study-Guide.png)

After completing the proficient study guide, I started to create another one for the basic material. However, I was running low on time until the examination (2 weeks), and did not finish this study guide. Instead, I switched my focus over to the practice quizzes and exam that were provided through the official CIA Learning System. I wasn’t particularly drawn to the practice quizzes, taking only a handful of questions from five sections within the first textbook (Essentials of Internal Auditing) before I decided to test my knowledge in the practice exam.

I went through the entire practice exam about a week before my test date, and scored a 59%. Far from a passing score, I started to get nervous about my understanding of the information and ability to pass the exam. I reviewed the areas where I was weak, and skimmed the reading materials of these parts.

I also came across a Youtube channel by Arif Zaman under the name of [Stuployer](https://www.youtube.com/@Stuployer). Arif has created videos on all three parts of the CIA exam where he covers every section in lecture. I watched all of the videos within the first two sections, but did not watch the third lecture series. You can find his first lecture, the Foundation of Internal Audit [here](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eNIYyBE-VXA).

Lastly, as the good AI interested fellow that I am, I used OpenAI's Chat GPT-4 to help my studying. I found this to be extraordinarily helpful, as I was able to leverage it's generative AI abilities to explain why quiz or practice exam answers were right or wrong. Having a conversation with GPT-4 on Internal Audit concepts was really interesting, and I found that it provided great insight. I also would 'test' it and paste some of the practice exam questions in it and ask what the right answer was. To my surprise, it answered every question right, except one! As an example of a prompt, I wanted clarification around the difference between control, control processes, internal control, and control environment and it provided a great example:

![GPT-4-CIA-Question](/My-thoughts-on-the-Certified-Internal-Auditor-QISA-Challenge-Exam/GPT-4-CIA-Question.png)

Out of all of the studying materials used, I would highly recommend reading all of the provided materials, focusing on proficient sections, and leveraging a generative AI model to discuss quiz and practice exam answers, especially questions that you get wrong.

## Exam Experience

I took my exam at an in-person testing center in Washington D.C. I arrived precisely 30 minutes before my exam time, and checked in at the front desk. They allowed me to start my examination slightly before to my scheduled time (which was very nice of them because I didn't want to wait around) and I was escorted to my computer. I was provided noise cancelling headphones and a scratch pad and pen.

The format of the exam was as expected, comprising of multiple-choice questions with four options each. However, the questions were crafted in a way that tested not just knowledge, but comprehension and application, often leaving me deliberating between two seemingly correct answers It required you to use a manager mindset, and deduce the best (or sometimes worst) option available. There was an option to flag questions for review at the end, which I did. However, I finished with only 11 seconds left, so I wasn’t able to review any!

I was definitely worried about the amount of time remaining as I tackled the last questions, but was able to finish on time. After submission, my exam closed and I received a print out with the words "PASS". What a relief!
